Features Your App Should Have to Pass Apple Review

Passing Apple Review is not only about writing clean code. Many rejections happen because the app is missing basic trust, privacy, account, payment, or safety features.

Elina Hovakimyan

These are not always complicated, but they are easy to forget when teams rush to submit.

The key thing to understand is this:

Not every app needs every feature.
A simple offline app has different requirements from a marketplace, social app, AI app, or subscription product. But if your app has accounts, payments, login, user content, tracking, or permissions, Apple expects the experience to be clear, safe, and complete.

Below are the main feature groups your app should check before submitting to App Review.

1. Legal, Privacy, and Support Links

This is the foundation.

Every app needs a Privacy Policy. Apple requires all apps to provide a privacy policy link in App Store Connect, and Apple also says privacy policy and support links are required for all apps. These links must work and should not lead to placeholder pages.

Your Privacy Policy should explain what data your app collects, how it uses that data, whether third-party services collect data, and how users can contact you or request deletion.

If your app has subscriptions, payments, user accounts, user-generated content, marketplaces, or custom rules, you should also include Terms of Use or an EULA. Apple specifically says auto-renewable subscription flows need functional links to the Privacy Policy and Terms of Use.

What to include:

  • Privacy Policy
  • Terms of Use / EULA
  • Support contact or support page
  • Working links inside the app
  • Working links in App Store metadata

Good places to show them:

  • Settings
  • Profile
  • About screen
  • Paywall
  • Signup/login screen
  • App Store Connect metadata

A weak Privacy Policy or broken support link makes the app look unfinished. Apple does not need much reason to reject that.

2. Account Creation, Login, and Account Deletion

If your app allows users to create an account, you need to think about the whole account lifecycle.

Apple's guideline says that if an app supports account creation, it must also offer account deletion inside the app. Apple's account deletion guidance says users must be able to initiate deletion from within the app, and deletion should remove the account and associated personal data unless the developer is legally required to keep certain information.

This means a "Contact support to delete my account" flow may not be enough if the deletion option is hard to find or unnecessarily complicated.

What to include:

  • Create account
  • Login
  • Logout
  • Delete account
  • Clear explanation of what deletion means
  • Confirmation before deletion
  • Handling of active subscriptions or retained legal records

A strong structure is:

Settings → Account → Delete Account

If your app has accounts but no delete account option, this is one of the most predictable Apple Review risks.

3. Third-Party Login and Sign in with Apple

If your app offers third-party login, such as Google, Facebook, LinkedIn, X, Amazon, or another social login, Apple usually expects you to offer Sign in with Apple or another privacy-friendly equivalent login option. Apple's guideline says apps using third-party login services must offer another login option with privacy protections, including limited data collection and support for hiding the user's email address.

There are exceptions. For example, if your app only uses your own username/password login, Sign in with Apple is not automatically required. It also may not be required for certain enterprise, education, or business-account apps.

But for most consumer apps, the practical rule is simple:

If you have "Continue with Google," you should strongly consider adding "Continue with Apple."

What to include:

  • Email/password login, if needed
  • Sign in with Apple, if third-party login is used
  • A clear login flow
  • No forced login unless the app genuinely needs it
  • Logout option
  • Account deletion option

Apple also expects the Sign in with Apple button to follow Apple's design rules. You should not create a random custom Apple button that only vaguely looks correct. Use the official native component or follow Apple's button style guidance carefully.

common mistake

Using a custom Apple login button with wrong spacing, wrong icon, wrong colors, or inconsistent text.

4. Purchases, Subscriptions, and Restore Purchases

If your app sells digital content, premium features, subscriptions, credits, or unlocks, this becomes one of the highest-risk areas of review.

For auto-renewable subscriptions, Apple expects the app to clearly explain what users are buying, how long the subscription lasts, the renewal price, and what content or features are included. Apple's subscription guidance also says the app should include a way for existing subscribers to sign in or restore purchases.

What to include on the paywall:

  • Subscription name
  • Subscription duration
  • Price
  • What the user gets
  • Renewal behavior
  • Free trial terms, if any
  • Restore Purchases button
  • Privacy Policy link
  • Terms of Use link

What to avoid:

  • Hidden renewal terms
  • Misleading trial language
  • Hard-to-find restore option
  • Paywall buttons that make pricing unclear
  • Promising features that are not actually available

If your app has paid subscriptions, your paywall is not just a design screen. It is a review-critical compliance screen.

5. User-Generated Content, Chat, Reviews, and Community Features

If users can post, upload, comment, chat, review, message, or create public profiles, Apple expects moderation and safety features.

Apple's guidelines say apps with user-generated content or social networking services must include filtering for objectionable content, a way to report offensive content, timely responses to concerns, the ability to block abusive users, and published contact information.

This applies not only to obvious social networks. It can also affect marketplaces, community apps, dating apps, kids platforms, vendor platforms, forums, and apps with public reviews or messaging.

What to include:

  • Report content
  • Report user
  • Block user
  • Moderation flow
  • Community rules or Terms
  • Support contact
  • Filtering or review process for public content

If your app has chat between users and no report/block tools, that is a real review risk.

6. Permissions and Sensitive Data Access

Apple expects apps to request only the permissions they actually need.

If your app asks for camera, photos, microphone, contacts, location, health data, Bluetooth, notifications, or tracking access, the reason must be clear. Apple says apps need user-facing purpose strings that clearly and completely explain why the app needs access to the requested data.

What to include:

  • Clear permission purpose strings
  • Permission request only when needed
  • Manual fallback when possible
  • No unnecessary access requests
  • Explanation before sensitive permissions

For example, do not ask for location access immediately on launch if the user has not reached a feature that needs location. Do not ask for contacts if the app can work without them.

A vague message like "We need this to improve your experience" is weak. A better message explains the actual feature.

7. Tracking, App Privacy Labels, and SDK Disclosure

This group is easy to underestimate.

Apple requires developers to explain app privacy practices in App Store Connect, including data collected by third-party partners and SDKs. Apple's App Privacy Details page says developers need to identify the data they or their third-party partners collect unless it meets specific optional-disclosure criteria.

If your app uses analytics, crash reporting, ads, attribution, login SDKs, support chat tools, payment providers, or AI APIs, you need to understand what data is collected and declare it correctly.

If your app tracks users across apps or websites owned by other companies, you also need to handle App Tracking Transparency correctly.

What to include:

  • Accurate App Privacy labels
  • Review of all third-party SDKs
  • Tracking permission, if tracking is used
  • No fingerprinting
  • No hidden ad tracking
  • Privacy Policy that matches the real app behavior
dangerous mistake

Selecting "Data Not Collected" while using SDKs that collect analytics, identifiers, crash logs, or user behavior.

8. Privacy Manifest and Required Reason APIs

Apple now also requires more technical privacy declarations for certain APIs and SDKs.

Apple's privacy manifest documentation says apps and third-party SDKs need to describe the data they collect and the required-reason APIs they use. Apple also requires developers to provide approved reasons for certain APIs in the privacy manifest.

This is especially relevant for apps using third-party SDKs, analytics tools, storage APIs, device information APIs, or older dependencies.

What to check:

  • Privacy manifest exists where required
  • Required-reason APIs are declared
  • Third-party SDKs are updated
  • SDKs include their own privacy manifests when required
  • App Store privacy answers match the real technical behavior

This is not only a legal task. It is also a technical dependency audit.

9. Review Access, Demo Accounts, and Test Data

Apple reviewers need to be able to test the app.

If your app requires login, subscription access, vendor access, admin approval, location-specific behavior, or special setup, you should provide clear review notes and demo credentials. Apple's App Review guidance says apps should include demo account information when login is required, and apps should not be submitted with obvious bugs or incomplete content.

What to include in App Review Notes:

  • Demo email
  • Demo password
  • Steps to test important features
  • Any required location or test scenario
  • Explanation of paid features
  • Backend/service availability
  • Test payment notes, if relevant

Do not assume the reviewer will understand your product. Make it easy for them to approve it.

10. Complete App Experience and No Placeholder Content

Apple expects the submitted app to feel finished.

Placeholder text, empty screens, broken links, incomplete metadata, test content, fake buttons, or unfinished flows can cause rejection. Apple's review process covers the app, its content, in-app purchases, and related assets to make sure the experience is safe and trustworthy.

What to check before submission:

  • No Lorem Ipsum
  • No fake listings unless clearly demo/test content
  • No broken images
  • No empty required screens
  • No broken buttons
  • No unfinished onboarding
  • No dead links
  • No crashed flows
  • No unavailable backend

This is where many teams lose time. The app may technically work, but if it feels unfinished, Apple may reject it.

Final Apple Review Feature Checklist

Before submitting, check these groups:

legal & trust
  • Privacy Policy
  • Terms of Use / EULA
  • Support contact
  • Working links
account & login
  • Login
  • Logout
  • Delete account
  • Sign in with Apple, if needed
  • Correct Apple login button style
payments & subscriptions
  • Clear paywall
  • Subscription details
  • Terms and Privacy links
  • Restore Purchases
  • Accurate purchase behavior
safety & user content
  • Report content
  • Block user
  • Moderation
  • Community rules
  • Support contact
privacy & permissions
  • Clear permission explanations
  • App Tracking Transparency, if needed
  • Accurate privacy labels
  • SDK data audit
  • Privacy manifest
review readiness
  • Demo account
  • Review notes
  • No placeholder content
  • No broken flows
  • Backend available during review

The best way to pass Apple Review is not to treat these as last-minute fixes.

Build them into the product from the beginning.

Apple is not only checking whether your app works. It is checking whether users can trust it.
